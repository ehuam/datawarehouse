1. Beta and Risk Measure Recommendations

1.1. Historical Beta vs Beta

Historical Beta (HBETA) is a measure of the a company's return relative to the market using 60 months of prior data. Beta would measure a security's sensitivity to the market conditions.

We looked to compare HBETA or BETA predictive abilities by sector.

1.1.1. Historical Beta Prediction Ability by Sector

We did this by plotting stock returns (2008 price less 2007 price over 2007 Price) by sectors to determine if there was a relationship. For the Technology and Communication sector, historical beta was able to capture the direction but the dispersion of the points suggests it wasn't able to capture precisely the return. The companies with higher historical beta than the average did experience negative stock return in general. For the financial sector, historical beta had even more dispersion. Stocks with a higher beta generally did have negative return but those with lower historical beta closer to the market (~.6) was a coin flip. Energy stocks with a HBETA .8 std deviation units above the market, had a higher return. The transport industry had 1 std deviation unit of beta above the market as a whole and had a -22% return. Basic Materials, historical beta captured the direction and was able to explain the stock return. The points were on the line. Healthcare, historical beta was able to capture the general direction with stocks that had .5 std deviations unit higher than the market but also had more dispersion. For industrials, historical beta was able to capture the direction and the points were closer to the line for stocks with a historical beta one unit higher than the market. Consumer staples, historical beta was able to predict the negative relationship between .4 units and .9. Stocks with a .94 historical beta could not be used as a good estimate of how much the underperformance would be. Consumer discretionary stocks with a hbeta between .4 and 1.3 units captured the negative relationshp. Stocks outside those bounds outperformed the market. Real esate and utilities was similar to the transportation sector, clustering around .5 hbeta. The sector had -31% stock return.

This suggests that the real estate and transport sectors could be used as reference points to other indusry performance. Together they both sit on opposite ends of historical beta, real estate having a .457 units compared to the market and transport having near 1 unit above the market (.991). As reference points they can be used to determine how other stocks with similar historical beta performed. When looking at financial sectors we see stocks with a lower historical beta, .53 performed like the real estate sector but then it wasn't able to predict whether a stock's performance after that.

1.1.2. Beta prediction ability by sector

Tech communication industry Beta was able to better explain a stocks performance with the data points closer to the regression line, with the exception of those companies that had 1 beta unit, there were vertical points where it could have gone either way. FInancial sector performance was better explained with beta less than .75 and greater than 1.7. In between those, stocks could have gone in either direction with returns between 0% and -65%. The energy sector, beta was .88 could be used to explain a positive performance. The Basics Materials sectors behaved the same with historical and beta. Its performance could be explained by both. The healthcare sector beta better explained the stock's return and captured the direction between .6 and .9. Outside of that range, stocks could go either way. Industrial sector was well explained by the beta measure, having captured the relationship and having less dispersion. For consumer staples, beta didn't do anything to predict the direction or performance of a stocks return. For consumer discre, stocks with a beta higher than .8 it had predictive power. at .8 a stock could have gone either way.

What we see is stocks with 1 beta unit, it could have gone either way for all sectors as they are clustering vertically. However its important to note that basic material sectors behaved in line with both historical beta and beta. There wasn't much dispersion as compared to other sectors.

2. Systematic Risk vs Total Risk

Total risk is the decomposition into common factors with the remaining sources of risk being specific to that industry/stock. Total risk can be thought of as the difference between a risk free rate, such as treasure rates, and everything else. Systematic risk is a further decomposition of what can be attributed to the market/benchmark or factor movements that cannot be diversified away. They reflect different perspectives on portfolio management.

Total risk looks to see how much risk is coming from broad trends, the factors, and how much comes from individual stocks. It's purpose is to view volatility as a whole and does not care about benchmarks. Systematic-Residual risk looks to see how much of the movement in price in terms of the market. It looks to view the sensitivity of stocks to the economy–beta.

3. Which Factors best predict percent change between 2007-2008

What we sought to do at first was to review our given values. We knew that they were normalized but Barra traditionally normalized based on S&P500 membership. So we sought to test this out. Our results showed that the factor values were instead normalized by the Barra estimation universe, having a Standard Deviation of 1, with their relative influence determined by their capitalization.

What we sought to do was to replicate Rosenberg and Marath's claim that our regression lines would be improved with the addition of technical and fundamental factors over using strictly historical performance – the Bayesian baseline. What the results would tell us is if these claims held during the financial crisis. Rosengberg and Marath found that using factors that came from market observations (technical factors) they would get a 57% improvement in predictive power. Using only information from a company's financial statements, they would see a 45% improvement. However, they saw that a combination of both helped explain variance by 85% over just using historical price movement.

The stocks we reviewed were those in the Barra Estimation universe and with a size factor greater than 0. This left us with 97 stocks. We wanted to do an analysis by sectors but because of small membership we had to group them into 'super-sectors'. The super-sector we left out was "Healthcare and Staples" purely because of economic intuition. We felt this was a defensive sector and could be used as a reference point.

Of the factors, trading activity was the best predictor of future pricing. The other factors did not show statistical significance. The factors were distinct enough from each other given by our condition number of being under 30 – we had a value of 9 indicating there wasn't a strong multi-collinearity effect between the factors.

What our results showed that the Rossenberg and Mareth's claim did not during the financial crisis. Fundamentals and Technicals performed slightly worse than using market information alone.

4. Which is faster? CTE VS SUBQUERY VS VIEW

Our results showed that on average, they all performed the same. Which was more an indication of the SQL optimizing engine's performance.
